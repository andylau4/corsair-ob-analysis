I. Executive Summary
The Big Picture

This report analyzes Corsair Gaming, Inc. through three key frameworks from Bauer and Erdogan's Organizational Behavior: Organizational Structure and Change (Ch. 14), Communication (Ch. 8), and Diversity and Inclusion (Ch. 2).1 Corsair, a U.S. gaming hardware manufacturer founded in 1994 and headquartered in Milpitas, California, provides a compelling case study because it combines rapid acquisition-driven growth, intense competitive pressure, and short product lifecycles in a global market.4

Our investigation reveals three interconnected organizational challenges. First, Corsair's expansion through acquisitions of brands such as Elgato, Origin PC, and SCUF Gaming has produced a complex matrix-like structure that reduces organizational unity, creates unclear reporting relationships, and generates significant coordination costs.4 Second, this structural complexity directly drives communication breakdowns documented in employee reviews: low Glassdoor ratings (2.8/5), poor psychological safety, and absent informational and interactional justice.5 Third, while Corsair demonstrates measurable demographic diversity, persistent gender imbalance and cultural barriers rooted in the same communication failures prevent the company from realizing the full organizational benefits of inclusion.311

Together, these findings illustrate how structural decisions cascade into cultural and behavioral outcomes, and how each dimension reinforces or undermines the others.

A note on sources Unless otherwise stated via footnote, all organizational behavior concepts, frameworks, and theoretical definitions referenced throughout this report are drawn from Bauer and Erdogan's Organizational Behavior (Version 5.0, FlatWorld, 2024).1

Chapter 14
Organizational Structure & Change

Problems Identified

  • Matrix structure violates unity of command where employees report to multiple managers across functional and product lines, creating role ambiguity and power struggles.
  • Acquisition-driven expansion (Elgato, Origin PC, SCUF, Drop) layers subsidiary brand cultures onto an already complex structure, multiplying coordination costs.9
  • Decentralization paired with unclear reporting lines produces redundant decision-making and inconsistent execution across business units.
  • Each restructuring (for example the Drop consolidation) introduces new disruption — shifting relationships, uncertain job security, and communication gaps.9

Proposed Solutions

  • Invest proactively in integration planning before acquisitions are finalized, establishing clear authority structures and communication protocols upfront.
  • Clarify reporting hierarchies within the matrix to reduce role conflict and defining who holds final decision authority for each product line and functional area.
  • Implement structured coordination and communication (cross-functional liaisons, regular sync meetings) to counterbalance the fragmentation of semi-autonomous subsidiaries.
  • Establish clearer formalization of roles and decision authority within each subsidiary to reduce ambiguity. Formalization makes behavior more predictable, which is especially valuable when integrating multiple distinct brand cultures.
Chapter 8
Communication

Problems Identified

  • A "toxic" environment at Corsair weakens psychological safety, leading employees to filter out or withhold critical information due to a fear of punishment.5
  • Upper management subconsciously practices selective perception, unconsciously filtering out viewpoints that do not align with their existing beliefs.
  • Management's common poor listening signals to employees that their opinions are not valued, which damages the employee-organization relationship and increases the likelihood of turnover.5
  • Corsair’s complex matrix structure creates communication inefficiencies that lead to wasted time as messages travel through various organizational levels.5
  • A "very political" environment and a lack of transparency during mergers erode the baseline trust and informational justice necessary for organizational functioning.5

Proposed Solutions

  • Establish an independent board to protect interpersonal risk-taking and implement a non-disciplinary approach that rewards employees for bringing up constructive criticism.
  • Implement anonymous quarterly reviews to confront leaders with actual data regarding employee feelings, forcing the bias of selective perception to become a conscious and addressable process.
  • Require management to attend annual workshops on active listening behaviors – such as full concentration, follow up questions, and validation – to repair the lack of felt empathy reported by employees.
  • Prioritize communication quality by ensuring instructions are clear and simple. This communication should include a "why” (i.e. why this matters), and follow a protocol detailing the objective, deadline, and final decision-maker.
  • Enforce transparent monthly town halls where decision-makers directly explain the reasoning behind irregular changes that would affect employees, such as budget cuts or policy shifts, to build trust and stop harmful rumors.
Chapter 2
Diversity & Inclusion

Problems Identified

  • Persistent gender imbalance: 69% men vs. 31% women in the workforce,11 despite having measurable ethnic diversity (40% Caucasian, 26.5% Asian, 23% Hispanic, 6.5% Black).3
  • Similarity-attraction bias and unconscious bias contribute to underrepresentation, especially in leadership and technical roles.
  • Diversity without inclusion: demographic representation exists, but the toxic culture and absence of psychological safety disproportionately silence marginalized employees.12
  • Communication failures compound inclusion barriers: those already facing identity-based marginalization are hit hardest by the lack of voice.12

Proposed Solutions

  • Move beyond demographic metrics to genuine inclusion — ensure employees can bring unique aspects of themselves to work while being treated as organizational insiders.
  • Address unconscious bias through structured hiring, promotion, and evaluation processes that reduce the influence of similarity-attraction tendencies.
  • Rebuild psychological safety (the Communication fix) as a prerequisite for inclusion — without it, diverse voices remain silenced.
  • Recognize that structure, communication, and inclusion are interdependent: investing in all three simultaneously is the only path to real organizational effectiveness.
IV. Evaluation
So What Does This Tell Us?

Lesson 1: Structure Drives Behavior

Organizational structure is not a neutral backdrop, it is itself a powerful behavioral driver. Matrix structures create predictable organizational conflicts like conflict over authority and role ambiguity. Corsair's acquisition-driven expansion has multiplied these problems by layering subsidiary brand cultures on top of an already complex functional structure. Companies must invest in integration planning before acquisitions are finalized.

Lesson 2: Communication Is Structural

Communication failures at the organizational level are rarely about individual skill deficits. The pattern of poor listening, absent justice, and low psychological safety reflects structural conditions. Organizations must actively construct the conditions for effective communication — establishing norms, training managers, and building feedback mechanisms that protect those who raise concerns.

Lesson 3: Inclusion ≠ Representation

Inclusion cannot be reduced to demographic representation. Corsair's relatively strong diversity metrics exist alongside a workplace culture described as toxic and unreceptive. When psychological safety, equitable processes, and genuine responsiveness are absent, diverse employees are present but excluded from meaningful participation. Structure, communication, and inclusion are interdependent dimensions.
