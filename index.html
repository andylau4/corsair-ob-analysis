I. Executive Summary
The Big Picture

This report analyzes Corsair Gaming, Inc. through three key frameworks from Bauer and Erdogan's Organizational Behavior: Organizational Structure and Change (Ch. 14), Communication (Ch. 8), and Diversity and Inclusion (Ch. 2). Corsair, a U.S. gaming hardware manufacturer founded in 1994 and headquartered in Milpitas, California, provides a compelling case study because it combines rapid acquisition-driven growth, intense competitive pressure, and short product lifecycles in a global market.

Our investigation reveals three interconnected organizational challenges. First, Corsair's expansion through acquisitions of brands such as Elgato, Origin PC, and SCUF Gaming has produced a complex matrix-like structure that reduces organizational unity, creates unclear reporting relationships, and generates significant coordination costs. Second, this structural complexity directly drives communication breakdowns documented in employee reviews: low Glassdoor ratings (2.8/5), poor psychological safety, and absent informational and interactional justice. Third, while Corsair demonstrates measurable demographic diversity, persistent gender imbalance and cultural barriers rooted in the same communication failures prevent the company from realizing the full organizational benefits of inclusion.

Together, these findings illustrate how structural decisions cascade into cultural and behavioral outcomes, and how each dimension reinforces or undermines the others.

Chapter 14
Organizational Structure & Change

Problems Identified

  • Matrix structure violates unity of command where employees report to multiple managers across functional and product lines, creating role ambiguity and power struggles.
  • Acquisition-driven expansion (Elgato, Origin PC, SCUF, Drop) layers subsidiary brand cultures onto an already complex structure, multiplying coordination costs.
  • Decentralization paired with unclear reporting lines produces redundant decision-making and inconsistent execution across business units.
  • Each restructuring (for example the Drop consolidation) introduces new disruption — shifting relationships, uncertain job security, and communication gaps.

Proposed Solutions

  • Invest proactively in integration planning before acquisitions are finalized, establishing clear authority structures and communication protocols upfront.
  • Clarify reporting hierarchies within the matrix to reduce role conflict and defining who holds final decision authority for each product line and functional area.
  • Implement structured coordination and communication (cross-functional liaisons, regular sync meetings) to counterbalance the fragmentation of semi-autonomous subsidiaries.
  • Reduce supervisory span where possible to minimize exposure to unethical or inconsistent leadership practices.
Chapter 8
Communication

Problems Identified

  • Glassdoor rating of 2.9/5 across 218 reviews — only 29% of employees recommend the company and just 14% express a positive business outlook.
  • Absent informational justice (insufficient truthful information sharing) and interactional justice (lack of dignity and respect), eroding organizational trust.
  • Low psychological safety — employees self-censor rather than risk negative consequences, blocking honest upward communication.
  • Selective perception by upper management — employees report leaders don't "listen to anyone's input," leading to filtered, distorted information flows.
  • Lack of transparency during stressful acquisition periods causes employees to fill information gaps with potentially harmful rumors.

Proposed Solutions

  • Establish norms of transparency — regular, honest communication from leadership about company direction, especially during acquisitions and restructuring.
  • Train managers in active listening and upward feedback reception to combat selective perception and demonstrate that employee input is valued.
  • Build formal feedback mechanisms (anonymous surveys, skip-level meetings) that protect employees who raise concerns, rebuilding psychological safety.
  • Address structural root causes: clarify authority lines to reduce the role conflict that discourages honest communication in the first place.
  • Prioritize communication quality over frequency — research shows quality matters most for team performance.
Chapter 2
Diversity & Inclusion

Problems Identified

  • Persistent gender imbalance: 69% men vs. 31% women in the workforce, despite having measurable ethnic diversity (40% Caucasian, 26.5% Asian, 23% Hispanic, 6.5% Black).
  • Similarity-attraction bias and unconscious bias contribute to underrepresentation, especially in leadership and technical roles.
  • Diversity without inclusion — demographic representation exists, but the toxic culture and absence of psychological safety disproportionately silence marginalized employees.
  • Communication failures compound inclusion barriers: those already facing identity-based marginalization are hit hardest by the lack of voice.

Proposed Solutions

  • Move beyond demographic metrics to genuine inclusion — ensure employees can bring unique aspects of themselves to work while being treated as organizational insiders.
  • Address unconscious bias through structured hiring, promotion, and evaluation processes that reduce the influence of similarity-attraction tendencies.
  • Rebuild psychological safety (the Communication fix) as a prerequisite for inclusion — without it, diverse voices remain silenced.
  • Recognize that structure, communication, and inclusion are interdependent: investing in all three simultaneously is the only path to real organizational effectiveness.
IV. Evaluation
So What Does This Tell Us?

Lesson 1: Structure Drives Behavior

Organizational structure is not a neutral backdrop, it is itself a powerful behavioral driver. Matrix structures create predictable organizational conflicts like conflict over authority and role ambiguity. Corsair's acquisition-driven expansion has multiplied these problems by layering subsidiary brand cultures on top of an already complex functional structure. Companies must invest in integration planning before acquisitions are finalized.

Lesson 2: Communication Is Structural

Communication failures at the organizational level are rarely about individual skill deficits. The pattern of poor listening, absent justice, and low psychological safety reflects structural conditions. Organizations must actively construct the conditions for effective communication — establishing norms, training managers, and building feedback mechanisms that protect those who raise concerns.

Lesson 3: Inclusion ≠ Representation

Inclusion cannot be reduced to demographic representation. Corsair's relatively strong diversity metrics exist alongside a workplace culture described as toxic and unreceptive. When psychological safety, equitable processes, and genuine responsiveness are absent, diverse employees are present but excluded from meaningful participation. Structure, communication, and inclusion are interdependent dimensions.
